In this guided exercise, you will learn how to configure the Red Hat build of Keycloak to allow users to authenticate using an external social identity provider. By offloading authentication to providers like Google or GitHub, you reduce the burden of password management and provide a more seamless experience for your users.

Before we begin, ensure you have a running instance of the Red Hat build of Keycloak, access to the Keycloak Admin Console, and an account with your chosen identity provider, such as the Google Cloud Console or GitHub Developer Settings, where you can obtain your Client ID and Client Secret.

Our first step is to obtain the necessary credentials from your chosen identity provider. To do this, log in to the provider’s developer console and create a new OAuth 2.0 Client. You will need to set the Authorized Redirect URI to the value provided by Keycloak. This typically follows a specific pattern: https colon forward-slash forward-slash, followed by your Keycloak host, the realms path, your realm name, and finally, the broker endpoint for your provider. Once you have created the client, make sure to copy both the Client ID and the Client Secret, as you will need these in the next step.

Now, let’s configure the identity provider within Keycloak. Log in to your Keycloak Admin Console and select the appropriate realm from the top-left dropdown menu. From the left-hand navigation menu, click on "Identity providers." Use the "Add provider" dropdown to select your desired service, such as Google. Enter the Client ID and Client Secret you obtained earlier. Take a moment to verify that the Redirect URI displayed on this page matches the one you entered in your developer console, and then click "Add" to finalize the configuration.

Next, you may want to configure user mapping to ensure that information from the social provider, such as email addresses or names, is correctly stored in Keycloak. On the Identity Provider configuration screen, click the "Mappers" tab and select "Add mapper." From here, you can choose the specific attributes you wish to map, such as Email, First Name, or Last Name, and then click "Save."

Once you have completed the configuration, it is time to verify the integration. Open your application's login page or the Keycloak Account Console. You should now see a new button, such as "Sign in with Google," appearing on the login screen. Click that button and authenticate using your social account credentials. Keep in mind that if this is your first time logging in via this provider, Keycloak may prompt you to link the social account to a local user account or register a new one.

If you encounter any issues, here are a few troubleshooting tips. First, if you see an "Invalid Redirect URI" error, double-check that the URI in the social provider's console matches the one in Keycloak exactly. Second, remember that many social providers require your Keycloak instance to be served over HTTPS. If you are working in a development environment on localhost, ensure your browser settings allow the callback. Finally, verify that your API and scope permissions in the social provider’s console include access to the user's profile and email address.

You have now successfully configured the Red Hat build of Keycloak to delegate authentication to a social identity provider. Your users can now authenticate using their existing social accounts, while you maintain centralized control over their access and roles within your Keycloak realms.
